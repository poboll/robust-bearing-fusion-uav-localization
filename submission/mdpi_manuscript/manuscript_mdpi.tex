\abstract{Single-cycle bearing-only localization can fail catastrophically when a small subset of measurements is missing, biased, or grossly corrupted. This paper proposes a corruption-aware front end for passive multi-UAV sensing that combines consensus-style geometric seeding, trimmed iteratively reweighted Levenberg--Marquardt refinement, conditional common-bias correction, and an optional budget-constrained screening add-on. Across 3000 Monte Carlo cases, the strict catastrophic-failure rate at $0.2R$ drops from 0.293 to 0.040 in the outlier regime and from 0.273 to 0.068 in the mixed regime relative to least squares. Across 536 measured-data replay windows and 227 deadline-filtered replay windows, the method does not attain the best replay median but repeatedly contracts the 95th-percentile error tail, providing a reproducible front-end baseline for suppressing extreme cue failures in corrupted passive bearing fusion.}

\keyword{bearing-only localization; passive target localization; robust estimation; budget-constrained screening; multi-UAV sensing; corrupted measurements}

\section{Introduction}

Bearing-only passive sensing remains attractive for multi-UAV missions because it preserves low observability, reduces payload burden, and can still provide actionable target cues when active ranging or infrastructure support is unavailable \cite{avola2024uav,liu2025survey,kramaric2025comprehensive,gao2023scalable,li2025bearing}. In practice, however, the hardest failure mode is not lack of geometry alone. A one-shot localization cycle can fail because a few bearings are missing, heavily biased, grossly corrupted, or inconsistent with the rest of the set. Once those measurements enter the fusion step, the resulting position estimate may leave the local region in which it is still useful for downstream search, tracking, or handoff.

Recent UAV localization research has advanced quickly at the system level. Representative directions include tightly coupled integrated navigation, distributed cooperative localization, factor-graph or surveying-adjustment formulations, multi-condition constrained fusion, and learning-assisted cooperative inference \cite{yang2020tightly,dai2022uav,yang2024distributed,qian2025cooperative,li2025tcdrl,li2026formation,liu2026high,ding2026set,jia2024composite}. A second line of work focuses on bearing-only sensing itself, covering AOA-based formation positioning, path optimization, observability enhancement, CRLB-driven trajectory design, and passive node selection \cite{kang2024aoa,huang2025emcompat,xing2025node,zou2024revisit,chen2025variable,wang2025bio,dogancay2024bayesian}. These studies leave a current-cycle front-end problem open: given one fixed set of bearings, how should the estimator remain reliable when part of the set is corrupted?

This front-end problem is not trivial. Recent work on error bounds, dynamic bearing processing, dual-UAV filtering, bearing-only circumnavigation, underwater passive localization, and distributed passive sensing all show that corrupted angular information can dominate localization quality \cite{chen2025error,luo2025dynamicbearing,yin2025dual,dou2025moving,mei2024bearing,zong2025distributed,yu2025passive,wu2026bearing,zhou2026direct,liu2026sensing}. Yet the response is often to change the path, redesign the observer geometry, or invoke a heavier cooperative back end. Those are important directions, but they leave a practical gap at the front end of the sensing pipeline: a mission system often needs a robust answer now, before a new trajectory or graph optimization can be recomputed.

From a UAV-systems perspective, that front-end gap is concrete. In practical passive sensing, bearings may be produced by gimballed electro-optical payloads, direction-finding antennas, or compact angle-only perception stacks whose outputs are constrained by field of view, asynchronous triggering, varying pose quality, and limited uplink budget \cite{chen2025research,peng2024trajectory,zhou2026target,zhang2026multi}. During aggressive turns or gusty low-altitude flight, gimbal vibration, transient non-line-of-sight blockage, and delayed inter-UAV packets can drive the angular error far away from the small-Gaussian assumption used by many clean geometric models. A planner or downstream tracker cannot benefit from those measurements if the localization front end collapses as soon as a few bearings become unreliable. This motivates a UAV-facing front-end question: how much robustness can be recovered inside one sensing cycle before invoking a heavier back end?

The second motivation is methodological. A large fraction of the localization literature either emphasizes geometry optimization or assumes a sequential estimator that can smooth errors over time. Those directions are important, but they do not remove the need for a robust one-shot estimate when a mission stack must react immediately. Moreover, sim-to-real studies in UAV autonomy repeatedly show that apparently small disturbances in attitude, delay, and synchronization can produce large downstream degradation if the front end is brittle \cite{panerati2021learning,perezsegui2023bridging}. For this reason, this study expands the validation chain beyond static Monte Carlo to include a measured-data multi-view replay surrogate with real visibility and asynchrony, a deadline-aware replay layer that discards stale or late bearings before fusion, pseudo-physical replay, and a multi-vehicle PyBullet replay layer.

This paper studies one-shot planar multi-observer bearing-only localization under corrupted measurements. The target is the fusion front-end inside a passive sensing stack: a robust estimator suppresses failures caused by bad bearings, and an optional screening stage is activated when only a limited number of measurements can be fused or transmitted.

This problem definition leads to three concrete design requirements. First, the front end must remain numerically stable when only a minority of bearings are unreliable, because a tactical UAV system often has to act before a new trajectory or full graph optimization is available. Second, the method should degrade gracefully when a bandwidth or compute budget forces the system to retain only part of the current bearing set; this motivates treating screening as a conditional budget-management module rather than as a universal replacement for full-set fusion. Third, the evidence should move beyond static geometry, because in practice the usefulness of a one-shot cue is determined by whether it remains consumable after timing variation, attitude disturbance, and navigation error are introduced. The architecture and validation chain used in the rest of the paper follow these three requirements directly.

The main contributions are threefold:
\begin{itemize}
\item a consensus-assisted robust bearing-only estimator that combines multi-start geometric initialization, trimmed iteratively reweighted refinement, optional common-bias correction, and a consensus fallback;
\item an Optional Engineering Extension for fixed-budget screening, together with residual-only, reliability-only, geometry-only, and adaptive baselines that clarify when screening is useful and when all-sensor robust fusion should be kept;
\item a reproducible validation chain comprising 3000 Monte Carlo cases across five corrupted-measurement regimes, a 2285-case fixed-budget screening benchmark, 536 measured-data multi-view replay windows with real visibility and synchronization, a 227-window deadline-aware measured-data replay layer with arrival filtering and staleness constraints, pseudo-physical dynamic replay with delay, jitter, per-sensor bias, and motion-induced disturbance, and a 1103-case PyBullet multi-vehicle replay study.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~2 reviews related work. Section~3 formulates the corrupted-measurement localization problem. Section~4 presents the proposed estimation and screening framework. Section~5 describes the experimental protocol, Section~6 reports the results, and Sections~7 and~8 discuss the scope and conclude the paper.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Bearing-Only and Passive Localization}

Bearing-only and passive target localization remain active topics in UAV and distributed sensing research because they support low-emission observation and can be combined with cooperative formations \cite{li2025bearing,kang2024aoa,huang2025emcompat,xing2025node}. Recent studies investigate D-criterion and Bayesian-FIM trajectory planning \cite{zou2024revisit,dogancay2024bayesian}, CRLB-based variable-speed path design \cite{chen2025variable,peng2024trajectory}, observability enhancement with sensor-bias estimation \cite{wang2025bio}, and bearing-only control with experimental disturbance handling \cite{sui2024adaptive,dou2025moving}. Related developments also appear in underwater passive localization, wideband array localization, direct target localization, and passive source localization in other sensing domains \cite{mei2024bearing,zong2025distributed,yu2025passive,wu2026bearing,zeng2026three,zhou2026direct,chen2025research}. The common thread is that geometry matters, but corrupted or uncertain bearings can still dominate the final error.

\subsection{Robust Estimation Under Corrupted Angular Measurements}

The literature increasingly recognizes that bearing quality is heterogeneous. Error-bound studies, dynamic bearing-angle analysis, multi-array likelihood processing, and dual-UAV filtering all show that uncertainty handling is at least as important as nominal geometry under realistic disturbances \cite{chen2025error,luo2025dynamicbearing,hu2025cyclic,yin2025dual}. Classical least squares is usually too fragile in the presence of outliers, observer-pose uncertainty, or mixed corruption, while generic stochastic search methods are often computationally heavy or hard to interpret. Standard robust choices such as Huber estimation \cite{huber1964robust}, redescending M-estimation \cite{beaton1974fitting}, RANSAC consensus filtering \cite{fischler1981ransac}, damped Gauss--Newton / Levenberg--Marquardt updates \cite{levenberg1944method,marquardt1963algorithm}, and graduated non-convexity \cite{yang2020gnc} are therefore natural baselines, but they are rarely packaged as an explicit front-end block for fixed corrupted bearing sets in multi-UAV passive sensing. In particular, recent UAV papers still devote more attention to trajectory or formation design than to the behavior of the current-cycle estimator when a few bearings become severely unreliable \cite{li2025bearing,huang2025emcompat,zhou2026target}.

\subsection{Sensor Selection, Observability, and Budgeted Sensing}

The second topic relevant to this paper is sensing selection. Passive node selection, observability-aware planning, and FIM/CRLB-based trajectory design are well established in the broader literature \cite{xing2025node,chen2025variable,wang2025bio,zou2024revisit,dogancay2024bayesian,peng2024trajectory}. At the system level, collaborative UAV localization has also advanced through factor graphs, distributed inference, relative localization, and swarm-level cooperative frameworks \cite{yang2020tightly,yang2024distributed,qian2025cooperative,li2025tcdrl,li2026formation,ding2026set,zhang2026multi}. The present work targets a specific front-end layer inside those broader frameworks. It addresses a centralized one-shot decision: once a set of bearings has already been produced for the current cycle, should all of them be fused, or should a smaller subset be retained because the current quality is too uneven? That framing is closer to front-end measurement triage than to long-horizon planning.

\subsection{Validation Gap}

Another gap is validation style. Public UAV-systems papers are increasingly expected to move beyond idealized static geometry and toward simulation pipelines that reflect timing, attitude, and coordination effects \cite{panerati2021learning,perezsegui2023bridging}. This is especially true for Drones-oriented work, where the distinction between a generic localization algorithm and a UAV-facing sensing paper often depends on whether the validation includes multi-vehicle trajectories, communication limits, or deployment-shaped disturbances. The current study still stops short of hardware-in-the-loop or onboard field tests, but it narrows that gap through measured-data replay, pseudo-physical replay, and PyBullet multi-vehicle replay while keeping the contribution centered on a corrupted-bearing front end.

Table~\ref{tab:litposition} summarizes this positioning more compactly. The gap addressed here is not integrated navigation, distributed state estimation, or long-horizon geometry control in general. It is the current-cycle behavior of a bearing-only localization front end when the incoming angle set is partially corrupted and, in some cycles, budget-limited.

\section{Problem Formulation}

We consider one localization cycle with $N$ observing platforms. The true planar observer positions are denoted by
\begin{equation}
\bar{s}_i = [\bar{x}_i,\bar{y}_i]^T,\qquad i=1,\dots,N,
\end{equation}
and the target position is
\begin{equation}
p = [x,y]^T.
\end{equation}
The estimator does not assume access to a full navigation stack. Instead, it receives reported observer poses
\begin{equation}
s_i = \bar{s}_i + n_{s,i},
\end{equation}
where $n_{s,i}$ models pose error from an external navigation module such as VIO, INS, or SLAM. This assumption resolves the usual tension between GNSS-denied mission language and known observer poses: the present paper studies the localization front end, not the upstream self-localization module.

Each valid observer contributes one bearing measurement:
\begin{equation}
\theta_i = \operatorname{atan2}(y-\bar{y}_i, x-\bar{x}_i) + b_c + b_i + \epsilon_i + o_i,
\end{equation}
where $b_c$ is a shared frame-level bias, $b_i$ is a sensor-specific bias, $\epsilon_i$ is nominal noise, and $o_i$ represents occasional gross corruption. A measurement may also be missing because of detection failure, occlusion, or communication loss. For a candidate target position $p$, the predicted bearing uses the reported pose:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\theta}_i(p;s_i)=\operatorname{atan2}(y-y_i,x-x_i).
\end{equation}
The wrapped residual is
\begin{equation}
r_i(p,b_c)=\operatorname{wrap}\!\left(\hat{\theta}_i(p;s_i)-\theta_i-b_c\right),
\end{equation}
where $\operatorname{wrap}(\cdot)$ maps an angle to $(-\pi,\pi]$.

The paper studies a one-shot planar setting with three explicit scope boundaries. First, the estimator is centralized and uses the current cycle only; it is not a sequential tracker or a distributed fusion protocol. Second, the reported observer poses are treated as inputs, but pose uncertainty is injected into the benchmark. Third, the geometry is planar; the paper targets fixed-height or small-height-difference settings and leaves full three-dimensional extension to future work.

The experimental thresholds are normalized by the formation radius $R$. In the main Monte Carlo benchmark, $R=10$, so success at $0.1R$ corresponds to absolute error $\leq 1.0$, and catastrophic failure at $0.5R$ corresponds to absolute error $>5.0$. A stricter $0.2R$ boundary is reported separately as a tail-risk diagnostic rather than as a replacement for the main success/failure thresholds. Together, these thresholds distinguish locally usable cues from clearly misleading ones while still exposing heavy-tail behavior.

\subsection{Operational Constraint Model}

The manuscript treats measurement screening as an engineering response to limited fusion budget rather than as a universal replacement for all-sensor estimation. Let $z_i\in\{0,1\}$ indicate whether bearing $i$ is transmitted or fused. Then the budgeted front-end problem is
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i=1}^{N} z_i \leq k,\qquad 3 \leq k < N,
\end{equation}
where $k$ reflects a fixed communication, computation, or timing budget for the current cycle. In practice, this budget corresponds to cases such as delayed uplink from a subset of vehicles, reduced onboard processing time, or a front-end module that is allowed to keep only a limited number of bearings before passing a candidate target cue downstream. A representative example is a 10-UAV round in which a TDMA uplink window can return only five or six bearing packets before the current fusion cycle expires, which is why the experiments use a budget close to 55\% of the valid set. The baseline against which screening should be judged is therefore not ``all-sensor robust fusion is always worse'', but rather ``when a subset must be used, can the subset be chosen more intelligently than with naive rules?''.

\subsection{Corruption Taxonomy and Applicability}

The expanded benchmark distinguishes four practically important error sources. First, \emph{outliers} model gross bearing corruption produced by spurious detections, local clutter, or momentary payload faults. Second, \emph{missing observations} model occlusion, field-of-view truncation, or communication dropouts. Third, \emph{shared or heterogeneous bias} models heading-reference mismatch, platform-frame misalignment, or per-sensor directional bias. Fourth, \emph{observer-pose uncertainty} represents the fact that the localization front end depends on an upstream navigation subsystem whose errors propagate into the current localization cycle. These are still abstractions, but they map more cleanly to the UAV sensing stack than a single isotropic-noise assumption.

\subsection{Application Scenarios and User Pain Points}

Three application scenarios motivate the current scope. The first is electromagnetically silent or low-emission cooperative observation, where several UAVs observe a noncooperative target without active ranging \cite{li2025bearing,huang2025emcompat}. In this setting, the operator's pain point is not only global observability. The immediate problem is that a small number of bad bearings can move the fused cue far enough that a downstream confirmation camera or interceptor will search the wrong neighborhood. A front end that suppresses those large one-shot failures is therefore valuable even before one discusses long-horizon formation control.

The second scenario is communication-limited swarm cueing. In many practical stacks, not every vehicle can upload every measurement at full rate, especially when edge compute, bandwidth, or synchronization budget is limited. This is the use case behind the optional screening module. The question is not whether subset selection is universally superior to all-sensor fusion. The question is whether, once a subset must be retained, the system can avoid obviously poor triage decisions. Framed this way, the measurement-screening module addresses a concrete engineering bottleneck rather than an abstract optimization game.

The third scenario is degraded navigation and asynchronous payload operation. Passive bearings are often produced by vision, gimbal, or direction-finding modules whose timestamps, headings, and internal quality are not perfectly aligned. At the same time, the observer poses delivered by GNSS, VIO, INS, or SLAM are themselves imperfect. This coupling between pose uncertainty and bearing corruption is precisely where plain least squares tends to become brittle. The present paper therefore focuses on the narrow question of what can be recovered inside one cycle before a tracker, planner, or human operator receives the cue.

\section{Materials and Methods}

\subsection{Framework Overview}

The proposed framework has two stages. Stage~1 is a robust localization core. It receives all currently valid bearings, generates several candidate target hypotheses, refines them under trimmed iteratively reweighted estimation, and keeps the hypothesis with the smallest final residual. Stage~2 is optional. When only a limited number of bearings can be fused or transmitted, it screens the current set using geometry and residual reliability. An adaptive gate bypasses screening and keeps all-sensor robust fusion when the current bearing set looks sufficiently homogeneous.

This decomposition treats localization as an engineering front-end problem. The estimator sits between navigation/bearing extraction modules and downstream planning or tracking modules, and it is designed to remain lightweight enough for repeated use within the current sensing cycle.

\subsection{Consensus-Assisted Robust Estimation}

\paragraph{Geometric candidate generation.}
For observer $i$, the bearing ray is
\begin{equation}
\ell_i(\tau_i)=s_i+\tau_i
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta_i\\
\sin\theta_i
\end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Pairwise ray intersections generate an initial candidate pool. The implementation keeps several consensus-style representatives of that pool, including the centroid, the coordinate-wise median, and a trimmed centroid. In addition, a sampled consensus prior is extracted so that heavily contaminated cases have an explicit fallback hypothesis before iterative refinement begins.

\paragraph{Robust objective and Jacobian.}
For active measurements, the estimator minimizes
\begin{equation}
\min_{p,b_c}\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i\,\rho\!\left(r_i(p,b_c)\right),
\end{equation}
where $\rho(\cdot)$ is implemented through iteratively reweighted least squares and the weights are updated after trimming large residuals. Let
\begin{equation}
d_i^2=(x-x_i)^2+(y-y_i)^2.
\end{equation}
The bearing Jacobian with respect to the target position is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_i(p)=
\begin{bmatrix}
-\dfrac{y-y_i}{d_i^2} &
\dfrac{x-x_i}{d_i^2}
\end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The code uses a damped Gauss--Newton/Levenberg--Marquardt step rather than finite-difference gradient descent. With $A_i=[\mathbf{J}_i\;\;-1]$ when common-bias correction is active, the linearized update solves
\begin{equation}
\Delta = -\left(A^TWA+\lambda I\right)^{-1}A^TWr,
\end{equation}
where $W$ is the diagonal matrix of current robust weights and $\lambda$ is a small damping term.

\paragraph{Trimmed iteratively reweighted refinement.}
The robust core updates weights from the residual pattern, trims the largest residuals, and then refines the estimate by several damped Gauss--Newton steps. Redescending downweighting is used after trimming so that the tail of the residual distribution is suppressed more aggressively than in plain Huber IRLS. The common-bias term is updated only when the active residuals are sufficiently concentrated; otherwise the algorithm falls back to zero bias rather than forcing an unstable common correction onto heterogeneous measurements. After refinement, a consensus fallback is retained when the residual tail remains too heavy or when the trimmed solution drifts away from the consensus hypothesis without a clear residual advantage. In this sense, the proposed estimator is a compact robust front end that combines geometric hypotheses, trimming, reweighting, and consensus rescue.

\paragraph{Algorithmic summary.}
The robust estimator can be summarized as follows. First, generate a pool of pairwise-intersection hypotheses together with consensus-style summary points. Second, evaluate a sampled consensus fallback so that a heavily corrupted case still has a viable initial anchor. Third, refine each candidate by several rounds of trimmed reweighting and damped Gauss--Newton updates, optionally correcting a shared bias when the active residuals are concentrated enough. Fourth, retain the candidate with the lowest final robust residual. Fifth, if the residual tail after refinement remains too heavy, fall back to the best consensus hypothesis. This sequence remains simple enough to reproduce from the codebase while still addressing the main failure modes seen in corrupted bearing sets.

\caption{Consensus-Assisted Robust Fusion}
\label{alg:frontend}
\begin{algorithmic}
\Require Reported observer poses $\{s_i\}_{i=1}^N$, current valid bearings $\{\theta_i\}_{i=1}^N$
\Ensure Current-cycle target cue $\hat{p}$
\State Generate pairwise-intersection hypotheses and consensus summary points
\State Form a small set of smooth hypotheses and one consensus-style fallback seed
\If{the coherence shortcut in Table~\ref{tab:stagerules} is satisfied}
    \State Return the smooth candidate with the lowest residual
\EndIf
\ForAll{geometric candidates and the consensus candidate}
    \State Activate shared-bias correction only if the bias rule in Table~\ref{tab:stagerules} is satisfied

\ElsIf{the smooth-rescue rule in Table~\ref{tab:stagerules} is triggered}

\Else

\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}

\subsection{Reliability-Adaptive Measurement Screening}

The screening stage starts from the pilot estimate delivered by Stage~1. Residual reliability is computed from the current residuals, and screening is invoked only in fixed-budget settings or when the adaptive gate considers the current residual pattern too heterogeneous to trust all measurements equally.

For a candidate subset $\mathcal{S}$, the weighted geometry matrix is
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{\mathcal{S}}=\sum_{i\in\mathcal{S}} w_i\,\mathbf{J}_i^T\mathbf{J}_i,
\end{equation}
and the isotropy term is
\begin{equation}
\eta_{\mathcal{S}}=\frac{\lambda_{\min}(\mathbf{F}_{\mathcal{S}})}{\lambda_{\max}(\mathbf{F}_{\mathcal{S}})}.
\end{equation}
The proposed normalized screening score is
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Score}(\mathcal{S})=
0.34\,\phi_{\det}(\mathcal{S})
+0.18\,\eta_{\mathcal{S}}
+0.24\,\bar{w}_{\mathcal{S}}
+0.24\,\phi_{r}(\mathcal{S}),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\det}(\mathcal{S}) = 1-\exp\!\left(-10^4\det(\mathbf{F}_{\mathcal{S}})\right),
\qquad
\phi_r(\mathcal{S}) = 1-\min\!\left(\frac{\bar{r}_{\mathcal{S}}}{0.25},1\right),
\end{equation}
and $\bar{w}_{\mathcal{S}}$ and $\bar{r}_{\mathcal{S}}$ denote the mean reliability and mean absolute residual on the subset. The score uses four normalized ingredients and avoids stacking several strongly correlated geometry terms.

The two geometry terms can be interpreted as local FIM surrogates. Specifically, $\phi_{\det}$ plays the role of a normalized D-optimality proxy and $\eta_{\mathcal{S}}$ behaves like a normalized E-optimality or isotropy proxy, both of which are commonly used in observability-aware trajectory and node-selection studies \cite{zou2024revisit,dogancay2024bayesian,chen2025variable}. The remaining two terms inject current-cycle credibility information that those geometry-only criteria do not capture.

The score approximates a small budgeted search problem. In idealized form, the screening objective can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S}^{\star}=
\arg\max_{|\mathcal{S}|=k}
\Big[
\alpha_1 \phi_{\det}(\mathcal{S})
+\alpha_2 \eta_{\mathcal{S}}
+\alpha_3 \bar{w}_{\mathcal{S}}
+\alpha_4 \phi_r(\mathcal{S})
\Big],
\end{equation}
with fixed coefficients $\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_4$ chosen once and then held constant across the reported experiments. The implementation evaluates this objective over a capped reliability-sorted pool of size $m=\min(N,\max(k+2,8))$ so that the search remains computationally small while still exploring more than the final subset size.

The proposed policy performs a small exhaustive search over a top-reliability candidate pool, which is feasible because the pool is capped in the experiments. Baselines include random subset selection, angular-spread greedy selection, FIM-only greedy selection, residual-only top-$k$, reliability-only top-$k$, and the all-sensor robust estimator. The adaptive policy uses all-sensor robust fusion when the residual median absolute deviation and reliability dispersion are both small; otherwise it activates screening. This makes screening a conditional tool rather than a default law. Section~6 therefore reports both score-term ablation and explicit coefficient perturbation so that the selector is not justified by one frozen coefficient vector alone.

\begin{algorithm}[!htbp]
\caption{Adaptive Budget Screening}
\label{alg:screening}
\begin{algorithmic}
\Require Pilot estimate $\hat{p}$, residuals $\{r_i\}$, reliabilities $\{w_i\}$, current budget $k$
\Ensure Subset $\mathcal{S}$ or the all-sensor decision
\State Compute residual dispersion and reliability dispersion on the current window
\If{the window is homogeneous and the full set can be fused}
    \State Keep all current valid bearings
\Else
    \State Form a capped candidate pool by reliability ranking
    \State Enumerate budget-feasible subsets in that pool
    \State Score each subset with normalized geometry, isotropy, reliability, and residual terms
    \State Return the highest-scoring subset
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Cue-usability metadata.}
Besides the coordinate cue $\hat{p}$, the front end exposes three lightweight diagnostics to downstream modules: the residual median absolute deviation, the local isotropy proxy $\eta$, and the active-bearing fraction after trimming. In deployment, these quantities can be combined into a simple usability score or passed directly to a tracker for gating and downweighting. These metadata are engineering diagnostics rather than calibrated probabilities, and they help a downstream consumer distinguish a numerically available cue from one that is geometrically weak or residual-inconsistent.

\subsection{Complexity and Reproducibility}

The geometric candidate generation stage is $O(N^2)$ because it depends on pairwise intersections. Each inner Gauss--Newton update is linear in the number of active measurements, while the number of outer reweighting rounds is fixed by configuration. In the screening stage, the exhaustive search is confined to a small top-reliability pool, so the practical cost is bounded by $\binom{m}{k}$ with small $m$ rather than by exhaustive search over the full sensor set. Error amplification follows the same geometry logic: when the effective information matrix becomes ill-conditioned, small angular perturbations can expand into large position errors roughly in proportion to $1/\lambda_{\min}(\mathbf{F})$. This is why the front end monitors both residual dispersion and isotropy instead of trusting geometry or residual size alone. All experiments reuse the same Python package interfaces, and all manuscript figures are generated from frozen JSON outputs rather than from manually edited spreadsheets.

\subsection{Implementation Details and Default Settings}

The implementation uses a trim ratio of 0.15, three outer reweighting rounds, Huber scale 0.08, Tukey cutoff 0.18, and LM damping $10^{-3}$ as default settings. The adaptive gate keeps all-sensor fusion when the residual median absolute deviation is below 0.05 and the reliability dispersion is below 0.12. The four screening coefficients are fixed after a small validation sweep and then held constant as a tractable surrogate for the budgeted geometry--credibility trade-off studied here. The fixed-budget screening benchmark sets the budget to approximately 55\% of currently valid bearings, which mimics a short TDMA-style uplink window in which only five or six out of ten observers can forward cues within one localization cycle. The exhaustive search is confined to a capped top-reliability pool. These settings are not retuned from regime to regime; they are fixed across the expanded experimental matrix. Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams} lists the exact values together with their engineering interpretation and the experiments in which they were stress-tested.

During the preparation of this manuscript, ChatGPT was used exclusively for language polishing. All technical methodologies, numerical claims, and results were independently verified by the authors.

\section{Experimental Protocol}

\subsection{Main Monte Carlo Benchmark}

The main synthetic benchmark contains 3000 cases. Five corrupted-measurement regimes are evaluated: clean, outlier, mixed, pose uncertainty, and heterogeneous bias. Each regime is run over 200 random seeds and three formation families (circular, random, and near-degenerate narrow-arc layouts) with eight observers. The formation radius is fixed at $R=10$, but the target is randomized reproducibly inside the formation footprint rather than fixed at one convenient coordinate. This removes dependence on one nominal geometry while preserving exact reproducibility. If a practical swarm operates with a 100~m observation radius, the normalized thresholds used in this paper map to 10~m at $0.1R$ and 50~m at $0.5R$. The outlier regime uses outlier rate $0.28$ and scale $0.42$. The mixed regime combines common bias $0.04$, missing rate $0.20$, outlier rate $0.22$, and outlier scale $0.45$. The pose-uncertainty regime injects observer-pose noise of standard deviation $0.35$. The heterogeneous-bias regime uses sensor-specific bias standard deviation $0.05$ in addition to a small common bias.

The baseline estimators are nonlinear least squares, Huber IRLS, GNC with a Geman--McClure robustifier, and RANSAC. The proposed method is evaluated under the same interface and with the same reported observer poses. The main metrics are median error, mean error, 90th-percentile error, success at $0.1R$, and catastrophic failure above $0.5R$. A stricter $0.2R$ catastrophic threshold is reported separately as a supplemental heavy-tail metric. Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals are reported for the medians.

\subsection{Fixed-Budget Screening Benchmark}

The screening benchmark contains 2285 valid fixed-budget cases over mixed and severe regimes, four formation families (circle, perturbed, random, and near-degenerate), and 8/10/12-observer settings. Cases with fewer than four valid bearings after corruption are discarded because fixed-budget subset selection is not meaningful there. The subset budget is approximately 55\% of the currently valid measurements, clipped to remain strictly smaller than the full set. In concrete terms, this corresponds to retaining roughly five or six bearings out of a ten-vehicle observation round when uplink, onboard compute, or fusion latency forces partial transmission. The benchmark tests whether a budgeted policy can use the limited subset more effectively than simple residual-only, reliability-only, geometry-only, or random screening.

\subsection{Pseudo-Physical Dynamic Replay}

To narrow the gap between static geometry and UAV-like dynamics, a pseudo-physical replay benchmark is also included. The replay generator injects motion-dependent delay, per-sensor bias, attitude-jitter-like disturbance, missing observations, and outliers before the current fusion cycle is evaluated. Forty seeds, three formations, and three swarm sizes produce 720 dynamic cases over mild and harsh regimes. This layer remains simulation-based, but it is closer to flight-like corruption than a purely static Monte Carlo sweep.

\subsection{Public Real-Flight Trajectory Replay}

To strengthen the validation chain with a measured data source, a measured-data multi-view replay surrogate is added using dataset 3 from the \texttt{drone-tracking-datasets} collection \cite{albl2020reconstruction}. The dataset provides surveyed observer locations, manual multi-camera detections, cross-camera synchronization parameters, and an RTK target trajectory. Rather than treating those detections as direct onboard bearing measurements, the replay layer uses the measured trajectory, real visibility masks, and synchronization tables to rebuild current-cycle angle cues under real multi-view geometry. Concretely, the real inputs in this layer are the surveyed observer poses, measured visibility masks, cross-camera synchronization tables, and RTK target trajectory supplied by the dataset. Each replayed bearing is then reconstructed from the synchronized observer--target geometry by projecting the RTK target position into the observer frame and taking the azimuth implied by the synchronized time stamp, so the replay inherits measured geometry and measured asynchrony but not the original image-space detector noise. The reported outlier, bias, and pose perturbations are injected only after this reconstruction step, which keeps the stress-test corruption controlled and reproducible instead of conflating it with the dataset's raw annotation process. Camera 0 is used as the reference stream, 450 reference windows are sampled over the full sequence, and the current cycle is retained only when at least three observers remain visible after synchronization and visibility filtering.

Two replay regimes are used. The nominal regime adds mild pose uncertainty, common bias, per-sensor bias, and a low outlier rate. The disturbed regime increases pose uncertainty, timing jitter, and outlier severity. This produces 536 retained replay windows in total. The value of this layer is specific: it is not a field trial of the full UAV system, but it is substantially closer to measured target motion, real observer geometry, and real asynchronous visibility than the purely synthetic layers.

\subsection{Deadline-Aware Measured-Data Replay}

To make the measured-data replay layer more system-facing, a second pass is added in which only bearings that reach the current fusion cycle on time are allowed into the estimator. Starting from the same public replay windows, each valid bearing inherits a synchronization age from the measured cross-camera frame mapping and then receives sampled link and queue latency. Bearings are discarded if they are lost in transit, exceed a staleness budget, or arrive after the front-end deadline minus a small processing guard. The nominal deadline regime uses a 170~ms fusion deadline with a 28~ms guard, leaving 142~ms of usable budget; the disturbed regime uses 300~ms and 40~ms, leaving 260~ms. Across the original 536 measured-data windows, this filter retains 227 windows in total, with 152/257 nominal windows and 75/279 disturbed windows surviving the deadline gate. The corresponding mean on-time bearing counts are 2.78 and 1.97 per original cycle, while every retained window still satisfies the estimator's minimum three-bearing requirement. This layer tests a concrete systems question: how robust is the front end once the current cycle can only fuse the bearings that are both visible and on time?

\subsection{PyBullet Multi-Vehicle Replay}

To further reduce the gap between static geometry and UAV-like temporal disturbance, we also replay bearing measurements from a multi-vehicle PyBullet environment derived from the \texttt{gym-pybullet-drones} ecosystem \cite{panerati2021learning}. The replay uses circle and ellipse formations with 8 and 10 vehicles, warm-up phases, time-sampled trajectories, gust-like disturbance, delayed observer poses, attitude-induced bearing perturbation, yaw-defined sensor field of view with a payload mounting offset, soft field-of-view dropouts, missing observations, and slowly drifting common bias. The resulting benchmark contains replayed localization cycles over nominal and disturbed regimes. Although the current study still does not reach hardware-in-the-loop, this replay layer now serves as a dynamic synthetic replay layer that complements the measured-data surrogate \cite{perezsegui2023bridging}.

\subsection{Additional Generalization and Efficiency Analyses}

Beyond the headline benchmarks, the manuscript includes five auxiliary analyses. First, a formation-generalization study compares circular, elliptical, perturbed, and random observer layouts under mixed corruption. Second, sensor-count scaling evaluates 4/6/8/10/12-observer settings for both circle and random formations. Third, sensitivity sweeps vary outlier rate, common bias magnitude, and nominal noise level. Fourth, ablation, observability, and runtime studies isolate the effect of trimming, bias estimation, reweighting, geometry quality, and computational burden. Fifth, two selector diagnostics are reported in the main text: score-term ablation and randomized mild/moderate/strong coefficient perturbations for the normalized screening score. These analyses do not replace the main benchmark, but they are important for showing where the method is fragile, where it generalizes, and what computational price is paid for the robustness gains.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Robust Localization Under Corrupted Bearings}

Table~\ref{tab:mainregimes} and Figure~\ref{fig:storyregimes} report the main result: corrupted measurements cause clear failures for least squares, while robust front-end estimation materially reduces both median error and failure risk. In the outlier regime, the median error decreases from 1.1186 for least squares to 0.2108 for the proposed method. In the mixed regime, it decreases from 1.0015 to 0.4346. These are not marginal differences: they represent a shift from brittle one-shot localization to a front-end that stays usable after bad bearings enter the set.

The comparison against stronger robust baselines is also informative. Huber IRLS and GNC-GM are both much better than least squares, but they remain consistently behind the proposed front end in the mixed, pose-uncertainty, and heterogeneous-bias regimes. RANSAC is the strongest competing baseline in the current benchmark: the proposed estimator often matches it in median error across the hard regimes and exceeds it mainly on tail-oriented metrics rather than through universal center-error superiority. This pattern is consistent with the design of the method: consensus remains crucial under gross corruption, while the trimmed reweighting and bias-aware fallback keep one unified front-end pipeline usable when the corruption mode changes from one window to the next.

The pose-uncertainty and heterogeneous-bias results are particularly important for the scope of the paper. \subsection{Why Not Just RANSAC?}

RANSAC is the right baseline to confront directly because it is already very effective in pure gross-outlier regimes. The results above show that the proposed estimator does not materially outperform RANSAC in the outlier benchmark, and that is an important boundary condition for the paper. The motivation for keeping the proposed front end is different: the same pipeline must also remain stable when heterogeneous error mechanisms interact with gross corruption. In those regimes, the proposed estimator remains comparable with RANSAC in median accuracy while preserving one consistent refinement-and-fallback stack for the rest of the manuscript.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:frontend} uses an internal sampled-consensus prior inside Stage~1, but the external comparison remains non-circular. The internal prior is only one candidate inside a broader estimator that also evaluates smooth hypotheses, trimmed Gauss--Newton refinement, optional bias correction, and fallback logic. The external baselines are executed independently as standalone competitors with their own objectives and stopping rules. The manuscript therefore compares complete front ends against complete baseline estimators while also making clear that consensus itself remains an important ingredient.

Table~\ref{tab:ransacincremental} decomposes that point in the mixed-corruption regime. Pure RANSAC has median error 0.5241 and P95 error 2.4951. Adding LM polishing to the consensus inliers captures most of the median improvement, reducing the median to 0.4233 and P95 to 2.2843. The complete proposed pipeline is close to the polished RANSAC branch in center accuracy, with median 0.4267, but gives a slightly lower mean, P95, and $0.2R$ catastrophic-failure rate over the same 717 retained cases. This ablation therefore supports a bounded claim: the full pipeline improves substantially over an unpolished consensus hypothesis and is at least competitive with a simple RANSAC+LM repair under mixed corruption, while the strongest standalone RANSAC baseline remains an important reference rather than a defeated strawman.

\subsection{Measured-Data Multi-View Replay Surrogate}

The measured-data multi-view replay surrogate is built from surveyed observer positions, the RTK target trajectory, measured cross-camera synchronization, and real visibility masks from a public multi-view flight dataset \cite{albl2020reconstruction}. For each synchronized and visible observer--target pair, a surrogate planar bearing is reconstructed as the azimuth of the line segment from the surveyed observer position to the RTK target point after projecting the 3D displacement into the local ground plane. Real geometry, visibility, and synchronization are therefore preserved by construction, while outlier, bias, and pose-noise corruption are injected only after this deterministic bearing reconstruction to probe front-end tail behavior under controlled stress. This layer therefore sits between a clean public replay and a fully synthetic benchmark.

Table~\ref{tab:publicrealreplay} and Figure~\ref{fig:publicrealreplay} show a clear trade-off. These replay results show that the front end suppresses part of the extreme tail under measured asynchrony and visibility loss even when central replay accuracy remains comparable to or slightly behind stronger baselines.

\subsection{Formation, Observability, and Sensor-Count Generalization}

The formation study confirms that the proposed front end is not tied to one nominal geometry. Across circle, ellipse, perturbed, and random formations, the proposed estimator reduces the median error relative to least squares in every tested case (Appendix~\ref{app:suppresults}, Table~\ref{tab:formationextra}). The largest gain appears in the random formation, where the median drops from 2.1047 to 0.5768. The method is not uniformly best against every stronger baseline: GNC-GM remains competitive in some formation families, especially when the geometry is already favorable. The main advantage appears when corrupted bearings destabilize otherwise reasonable geometry.

Sensor-count scaling shows a similar pattern. The proposed estimator benefits substantially from moving from 4 to 8 or 10 observers, especially in random formations where the median decreases from 0.7221 at 4 observers to 0.4051 at 10 observers. The circle-formation scaling curve is also informative: the least-squares median is non-monotonic and can become worse at 12 observers under corruption, whereas the robust front end remains below 0.45 once eight or more observers are available. This suggests that additional corrupted measurements do not automatically help unless the fusion rule is robust.

The observability analysis adds an important nuance. Better isotropy still correlates with lower median error, but poor geometry alone does not explain the failures of least squares. Figure~\ref{fig:observability} shows that the proposed front end compresses the vertical spread of the error distribution over a wide isotropy range, meaning that it is addressing corrupted measurements rather than merely exploiting easy geometry.

\subsection{PyBullet Multi-Vehicle Replay}

The PyBullet replay layer exposes the transfer gap more clearly than the purely synthetic benchmarks. Table~\ref{tab:pybulletparams} summarizes the replay configuration, including update rate, delay distribution, attitude-induced perturbation, and gust disturbance. Over 1103 replayed localization cycles, the overall median errors are 1.7469 for least squares, 1.6967 for GNC-GM, 2.0073 for RANSAC, and 2.0611 for the proposed estimator (Appendix~\ref{app:suppresults}, Table~\ref{tab:pybullet}). The proposed front end therefore does not dominate replay median accuracy or replay catastrophic-failure rate. Its main signal in this layer is tail-oriented: the mean error drops from 50.61 for least squares to 21.20, the overall 90th percentile drops from 16.69 to 13.11, and under disturbed replay the 90th percentile drops from 36.53 for least squares and 23.71 for RANSAC to 18.19 for the proposed method.

\subsection{Cue-Quality Partition Under Replay}

Median error alone does not fully explain whether a replayed localization window is useful to the rest of the mission stack. To make the application story more explicit, Figure~\ref{fig:utility} partitions replay windows into three cue classes using the same error thresholds already defined in Section~3: \emph{handoff-ready} cues with error $\leq 1.0$, \emph{degraded} cues with error in $(1.0, 5.0]$, and \emph{unusable} cues with error $> 5.0$. This is still a proxy rather than a full task-loop experiment, but it is a better description of replay transfer than a single mean or median.

The cue-quality partition clarifies why the PyBullet benchmark matters even though it is not a universal win. Over the overall replay pool, the proposed method produces a handoff-ready fraction of 0.3028 and an unusable fraction of 0.2584, which leaves it inside the same broad operating band as the stronger robust baselines rather than clearly ahead of them. Under disturbed replay, the same boundary remains: the handoff-ready fraction is 0.2546 and the unusable fraction is 0.2848. The replay layer therefore exposes a real transfer gap: static synthetic robustness does not automatically become better cue availability once delayed poses, attitude perturbation, and field-of-view effects are introduced together.

The runtime results show that the robustness gain does not require an obviously impractical computational budget, although the method is clearly slower than simple least-squares fitting.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Operating Regimes and Tail Behavior}

The strongest evidence comes from outlier-rich, mixed-corruption, and disturbed-replay settings. In those windows, the main benefit of the proposed front end is not universal center-error dominance but heavy-tail suppression. A tracker, handoff module, or downstream search procedure can often tolerate moderate noise more easily than catastrophic cue displacement. The proposed estimator therefore adds value primarily by preventing a small number of corrupted cycles from dominating the mission outcome.

The baseline comparison supports the same interpretation. Least Squares is clearly too brittle once corruption becomes non-negligible, while GNC-GM and RANSAC already provide strong robust references. Against those stronger baselines, the proposed estimator remains comparable in center accuracy and stronger in several tail-oriented metrics under mixed corruption, heterogeneous bias, and disturbed replay. The same conditional reading applies to Stage~2: budgeted screening is useful when credibility heterogeneity is high or when only part of the current bearing set can be fused, whereas all-sensor robust fusion remains the correct default when the full set is available and coherent.

\subsection{Implications for Passive Multi-UAV Sensing}

Taken together, Figures~\ref{fig:systempipeline}, \ref{fig:publicrealreplay}, \ref{fig:deadlinereplay}, \ref{fig:utility}, and \ref{fig:trackingproxy} support a concrete system interpretation. The method improves the reliability of the current-cycle target cue before it is consumed by downstream modules such as tracking, handoff, or replanning. The measured-data multi-view replay surrogate strengthens this claim by exposing the front end to real observer geometry, visibility loss, and synchronization offsets; the deadline-aware replay layer then adds a cycle-level arrival filter that removes stale or late measurements before fusion; and the PyBullet replay layer adds multi-vehicle motion, delay, and attitude-induced perturbation. The downstream Kalman proxy then shows that reduced localization tail risk can translate into smaller tracking-error tails even when break-rate improvements are limited.

This validation chain also clarifies what the present study contributes to a UAV sensing pipeline. The paper isolates a front-end failure mode that frequently appears before longer-horizon modules can help: a partially corrupted set of bearings must still be fused into a usable cue inside the current cycle. The resulting contribution is a current-cycle robust-localization block with explicit interfaces to upstream poses and bearings and to downstream tracking, handoff, or replanning modules.

\subsection{Limitations and Next Steps}

Several limitations remain. The current formulation is planar and centralized, and the observer poses are assumed to be supplied by an external navigation module rather than estimated jointly with the target. Although pose uncertainty is injected into the benchmark, the estimator does not yet use covariance-aware weighting or a joint uncertainty propagation model inside the refinement step. The screening score is still a fixed engineering surrogate rather than a learned or theoretically optimal budgeted selector. The validation chain now includes measured-data replay, deadline-aware measured-data replay, pseudo-physical replay, and PyBullet replay, but it still stops short of SITL, HITL, or onboard field validation.

These limitations point directly to the next development steps. The most immediate extension is to replace the current pose-agnostic weighting with covariance-aware refinement so that navigation uncertainty is handled inside the estimator rather than only in the benchmark. A second extension is to move from the current planar formulation to full three-dimensional bearing fusion with altitude variation and camera or antenna mounting geometry. A third extension is system-level integration: coupling the present front end to richer trackers, explicit communication scheduling, and onboard companion-computer implementations. The reported desktop timings show that the current Python prototype is already usable for algorithm studies, but practical airborne deployment will likely require C++ reimplementation or parallelization on the onboard companion computer.

\section{Conclusions}

This paper formulates the contribution as a corruption-aware one-shot bearing-only localization problem and presents a robust front end for that problem. The consensus-assisted estimator suppresses catastrophic one-shot failures caused by bad bearings, while the optional adaptive screening add-on improves over simpler fixed-budget subset policies without replacing all-sensor fusion when the full measurement set is available. Across 3000 Monte Carlo cases, 536 measured-data multi-view replay windows, 227 deadline-filtered replay windows, and 720 pseudo-physical dynamic replay cases, the method consistently improves over least squares. Against the strongest robust baselines, the evidence is more bounded: in measured-data replay, Least Squares and GNC-GM retain the best medians in the nominal and disturbed subsets, while in deadline-aware replay the strongest baselines still set several center-error values, but the proposed front end repeatedly produces the smallest upper tail; in PyBullet replay, median accuracy remains comparable while tail-oriented tracking metrics improve in disturbed windows.

The main value of the study is therefore a reproducible tail-risk suppression baseline for passive bearing-only localization under outliers, mixed corruption, observer-pose uncertainty, and heterogeneous bias. Future work should add covariance-aware refinement, extend the formulation to full three-dimensional localization, connect the front-end output to richer downstream task metrics, and validate the pipeline through SITL, HITL, or onboard experiments.
